## Conservation Science and Biodiversity Impacts

Our lab translates macroecological theory and large-scale biodiversity data into tools and insights that inform conservation planning, protected area design, and policy. We work with government agencies, NGOs, and international partners to apply trait-based models, species distribution forecasts, and synthesis databases to real conservation challenges.

---

### Bridging Biodiversity and Conservation Science

Brian Enquist is co-director of the **Bridging Biodiversity and Conservation Science** group, a collaborative initiative linking quantitative ecology, biodiversity informatics, and applied conservation. The group develops frameworks that connect mechanistic ecological theory with on-the-ground conservation decision-making.

Core aims:
- Identify where biodiversity is most vulnerable to climate and land-use change
- Provide spatially explicit forecasts for protected area prioritization
- Build reproducible workflows that conservation practitioners can apply at national and regional scales

---

### SPARC: Spatial Priorities for Species in Response to Climate Change

SPARC is a collaboration involving **Conservation International** and international university partners to identify conservation priorities under current and future climate scenarios. The objective is to reduce extinction risk while sustaining ecosystem services.

SPARC integrates:
- Species distribution models across tens of thousands of plant and animal taxa
- Climate-change vulnerability assessments
- Protected area gap analyses at continental scale
- Economic and social co-benefit layers

### Trait-Based Vulnerability Assessment

We use **Trait Driver Theory (TDT)** and functional trait databases (BIEN, TRY, OpenTraits) to assess which species and communities face the greatest physiological risk from warming, drought, and land-use change. Trait-based vulnerability metrics offer a mechanistic alternative to purely correlative range models and can be applied even for data-poor taxa.

Applications include:
- Identifying thermally vulnerable plant communities in the tropics
- Quantifying drought sensitivity gradients across the Americas
- Flagging trait-mismatch risk for species nearing environmental limits

### BIEN for Conservation Planning

The [BIEN project](https://biendata.org/) provides the largest standardized database of plant occurrences, traits, and plot inventories for the Western Hemisphere. BIEN data are actively used in:

- National biodiversity strategies and reporting (CBD, IPBES)
- Protected area effectiveness assessment
- Regional flora prioritization (Amazon, Andes, Mesoamerica)
- Invasive species range projections

### Long-Term Monitoring for Adaptive Management

Our long-term forest censuses — including the **San Emilio Forest Dynamics Plot** in Costa Rica and the **CHAMBASA** network in Amazonia — provide baseline data for assessing ecosystem change and informing adaptive management. Repeated censuses document shifts in composition, structure, and function under changing climate regimes.
